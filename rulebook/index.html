# Military Context

ⓘ For more historical background which is not indispensable for gameplay, visit the history corner!

The Toyota Wars is a two-player simulation of a series of military conflicts that played out between 1983-1987 in the country of Chad. These conflicts included a civil war, the overthrowing of Goukouni's government by commander Hissène Habré, and the invasion of the country by neighboring Libya under the leadership of Muammar Gaddafi.

The term "Toyota Wars," "Toyota War," or "Great Toyota War" is sometimes used to refer to a phase of Chadian civil war in 1982-87, and other times is used to refer to the final phase of the Chadian-Libyan conflict (opens new window), which lasted from 1978 to 1987.

This game includes elements of both conflicts, but focuses primarily on the civil war between Hissène Habré (opens new window), Commander of FAN (Armed Forces of the North), and Goukouni Oueddei (opens new window), Commander of FAP (the People's Armed Forces).

# Commanders

Each player controls one of the two commanders in the civil war. Players will be referred to as the FAN Player and the FAP Player.

Libya, France, and the OAU (the Organization for African Unity), may intervene and assist either commander in various ways, but the larger political decisions on which intervention is based are not controlled by either player.

There are multiple possible scenarios to chose from when playing The Toyota Wars, each of them covering a specific period in the Chadian civil war, and, to a lesser extent, the Chadian-Libyan conflict. The full Strategy and Tactics game magazine (opens new window) contains all scenarios.

This rulebook focuses on the Opération Manta scenario, which covers a two-year period in the war between 1983 and 1984. This period of the civil war is characterized by the simultaneous invasion of Chad by Libyan forces and France's attempt at preventing such an invasion from toppling Habré's government in N'Djaména. The French intervention under President François Mitterand was codenamed Opération Manta (opens new window), or "Operation Stingray."

Below is a brief outline of France's and Libya's goals for intervention insofar as they impact gameplay:

  • The goal of Gaddafi's invasion is to get Goukouni in power in order to augment Libya's chances of controlling Chad once the civil war is over. If Goukouni succeeds in taking the government, Libyan forces remain loyal to him.
  • France's goal is to maintain political stability no matter which government currently controls her former colony. Such a policy allows France to continue benefiting from a privileged economic and military relationship with Chad despite its political ups and downs. However, in a Cold War context, France and the U.S. also suspect Gaddafi of being in a close relationship with the Soviety Union and want to prevent him from taking control of Chad.

In Opération Manta, Habré is the initial Government Commander, making the FAN Player the Government Player. During the course of the game, Goukouni may become the Government Commander by capturing the capital city, N'Djaména, with the help of the invading Libyans and allied guerrilla factions. This means that the FAP Player can become the Government Player.

# Combat Units

Below are all combat units which both Commanders — and hence both players — have at their disposal.

Combat Units are categorized as either Conventional Units or Guerrilla Units.

# Conventional Units

# Conventional Government Allies
  • FANT Government army. Although first recruited by Habré, these units come under Goukouni's control if he succeeds in taking control of the government.
  • MANTA French Marine and Air Force units from the Manta task force. Controlled by the government.
  • OAU Peacekeeping forces from the Organization for African Unity. Controlled by the government.
# Conventional FAP Allies
  • Task Force P Troops from the Libyan army and the Islamic Legion, sent by Gaddafi to aid his Chadian ally Goukouni. Libyan troups are specifically allied with Goukouni, whether he is in control of the government or not.

# Guerrilla Units

In addition to FAN and FAP, there are a total of 12 other Guerrilla Factions participating in the civil war. Their allegiance to one of the two Commanders will vary throughout the game.

Below are all guerrilla factions involved in Opération Manta.

# FAN and FAN-allied Guerrilla Allies
  • FAN Habré's personal army. Whether Habré is Government Commander or Rebel Commander, FAN remain loyal to him.
  • MPLT Allied rebel troops based out of Mao, Kanem, map hex 1631.
  • PLA Allied rebel troops based out of Mongororo, Ouaddaï, map hex 4239.
# FAP and FAP-allied Guerrilla Allies
  • FAP Goukouni's personal army. Whether Goukouni is Government Commander or Rebel Commander, FAP remain loyal to him.
  • CDR Allied rebel troops based out of Koro Toro, Bourkou, map hex 2826.
  • CODOS Allied rebel troops based out of Korbol, Moyen Chari, map hex 2446.
# Neutral
  • MRP "Inactive" guerrilla units based out of Moundou, Logone. These units do not engage in combat until their city of Moundou 1849is attacked. At this point, they are activated and fight against the attacker.
    However, the Government Player can attempt to rally them with a roll of the dice at beginning of each Tactical Phase.

COMBAT UNITS RECAP

The single most important thing to remember about guerrillas is that Habré and his allies are represented by Blue game pieces while Goukouni and his allies are Red.

When it comes to conventional troops, just remember these alliances: Green are allied with Goukouni , while Purple and White are allied with the Government Player, which, at the beginning of the game, is Habré.

If you take a quick look at the game pieces and decipher them using the key displayed above, you may begin to notice a few of the broad traits that characterize different armies and commanders involved. A few of these general characteristics are listed here:

  • Libyan forces are characterized by an abundance of tanks, mechanized units, and large infantry battalions with excellent conventional battle strength and movement speed.
  • FANT are not the most capable units, even in conventional warfare.
  • French troops, although they are conventional, can be surprisingly capable at guerrilla warfare as well. Some players find that this combined skill makes the French ideal for guarding the capital city.
  • Habré is a much more able military commander compared to Goukouni. His skill, however, only benefits those units with whom he is physically present (see FAN Commander's Movement Bonus).
  • Habré's FAN troups are much more loyal than Goukouni's army. However Goukouni has the potential to command a much larger number of units, especially if he succeeds in captural the capital along with the government FANT troops.

# Game Map

↓ Click on the image below for a high resolution image of the game map

An updated version of the game map is provided with this Handbook. The map legend can be found here.

The game rules included in the Handbook are compatible with both the updated map and the original map.

A few of differences between the original 1991 game map and the updated map are listed here:

  • A number of charts on the original map have been moved to the Tables page in the Handbook.
  • In order to enter a Sanctuary on the updated map, units simply enter the Sanctuary Country, 2.5 on the map legend. For example, if an MPLT Rebel Unit wishes to enter the Nigeria Sanctuary, the Unit is simply placed inside Nigeria. For this reason, the updated map does not include the dedicated sanctuary boxes seen on the old map. The list of Guerrilla Factions allowed into each respective Sanctuary can be found directly in the Sanctuaty Country.
  • The dedicated sanctuary boxes on the legacy map include a New Arrivals section. On the updated map, the sanctuary entry hexes 2.4 serve the purpose of a New Arrivals section. In other words, the entry hexes do not merely indicate where units can enter, but they are also where the units should be placed upon Arrival. Once the units are no longer New Arrivals, they are moved into the Sanctuary Country proper 2.5.
  • The visual elements on the updated map legend are numbered (e.g. 1.1, 1.2...) so that they can be more easily referenced from the game rules. Ignore these references when using the legacy map.

# Sequence of Play

At the most basic level, the Sequence of Play for the Toyota Wars consists of alternating phases of Movement and Battle. At the highest level, gameplay follows alternating six-month seasons of military campaigning called Tactical Phases.

The basic Sequence of Play For Opération Manta can be visualized with the following hierarchy:

     TACTICAL PHASE 1
     |
     |
     |__ Impulse 1
     |   |
     |   |__ Government Segment
     |   |   |__ Movement
     |   |   |__ Battle
     |   |__ Rebel Segment
     |       |__ Movement
     |       |__ Battle
     |
     |
     |__ Impulse 2
     |
     |
     |__ Impulse 3


     TACTICAL PHASE 2

In reality, the Sequence of Play is a bit more complicated than this (see Gameplay: Sequence of Play), however, these are the main phases. Let's break down each one.

# Tactical Phases

At the broadest level, The Toyota Wars plays out as a sequence of Tactical Phases. Each Tactical Phase is characterized by different fundamental rules and conditions for Movement and Battle. For example, the first Tactical Phase of Opération Manta happens during Wet Season. During this time, wells are more available to units in the desert (see Season Change), but marshes are more troublesome to navigate in the savanna (see Terrain Effects Chart). The second Tactical Phase, on the other hand, happens during Dry Season. For this reason alone it can be useful to think of Tactical Phases as six-month tropical seasons.

# Impulses

Each Tactical Phase is split up into three Impulses. An Impulse is the basic "round" for Toyota Wars, during which each player plays one turn. We might say, then, that each Tactical Phase lasts three rounds — that is, three Impulses.

# Segments

During each Impulse, the Government Player plays first and the Rebel Player plays second. Each player's turn is called a Segment. For the government, a Friendly Segment is a Segment during which the government plays; for the Rebel Player, it is the Segment during which the Rebel Player plays. Since The Toyota Wars is a two-person game, there are only two Segments per Impulse.

# Movement and Battle

During each player Segment, the player first moves whichever unit they wish to move, and then proceeds to attack any enemy units within attacking distance. In other words, a player's turn can be split up into Movement and Battle.

SEQUENCE OF PLAY RECAP
Movement and then Battle make up a player turn, or Segment. Once each player has played, these two Segments make up an Impulse. Three Impulses are repeated in order to complete a Tactical Phase. Once a Tactical Phase is complete, the seasons change and the next Tactical Phase begins.

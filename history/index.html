# Military use of Toyota Land Cruisers

Task Force PFANT Toyota Land Cruiser J45 Pickup, 1968-1984



The original 1991 Toyota Wars magazine lists the following "Toyotas" used by both Chad and Libya during the war:

  • Chad
    • Renault TRM 2000
    • Renault TRM 4000
    • Toyota Trucks
    • Jeeps
  • Libya
    • Toyota Trucks
    • Jeeps
    • HD Mercedes 2432

The four-wheeled vehicles on this list — the Jeeps and Toyotas — were light enough that they would not set off a minefield if driven above 100km/h, yet rugged enough for off-road Sahara warfare.

While the above list specifies the models of the Renault and Mercedes transport trucks used, it does not specify that of the Toyotas. In the mid-1980s there were three major Toyota Land Cruiser lineups: the J40 Series (opens new window), the J60 Series (opens new window), and the J70 Series (opens new window). The principal difference between these lineups is that the J40 and J70 series were more rugged; the J60 was a more comfortable off-road vehicle. While the J40 was an older variant of the Land Cruiser produced since 1960, the J70 went into production beginning in 1984.

Toyota Land Cruiser J70 Series short-wheelbase chassis, 1984-2007


They were of course other Toyota trucks in circulation in the 1980s, such as the Hilux, but the Land Cruiser represented the more desirable vehicle for the military, especially in desert warfare.

Although all models of Land Cruiser and Hilux became popular for low-budget warfare in developing countries toward the end of the 20th century, it was the long-wheelbase Land Cruisers J75 and J77 — the latter rebranded the J78 post-1999 — that were especially favored for both their ruggedness and their spacious interior. To this day, the J77/78 is referred to as the "Troop Carrier," or "Troopy." Additionally, the J45 pickup and its successor the J79 are ideal for use as technicals, although these are often misidentified as Hilux. Specifically, it is the Land Cruiser J79 that occupied the status of "vehicular equivalent of an AK-47" from the late-1980s onward — and it still does as of 2020.

Land Cruiser J77 "Toop Carrier," 1984-2007

Despite the Chadian military's gradual acquisition of heavier armored vehicles over the decades following the Toyota War, the J70 Series remained an indispensable piece of equipment for several decades. In reporting on the Boko Haram insurgency of the 2010s, the J79 pickup can be spotted in nearly every photo of the Chadian military containing vehicles other than tanks. Most are post-2007 facelifted variants.

  • Military J79s on the Nigeria-Cameroun border in January 2015 (opens new window)
  • Military J79 on the Nigeria Cameroun border, February 2015 (opens new window)
  • Military J79 in Northern Nigera, March 2015 (opens new window)
  • Military J79 returning from a mission in Nigeria, January 2020 (opens new window)

Toyota Land Cruiser J79 Pickup, 1984-2007

Judging from sparse photos, Boko Haram may have had a larger percentage of Hilux pickups, rather than the more rugged Land Cruisers (see here (opens new window), here (opens new window), and here (opens new window)).

During the civil unrest in Libya following Gaddafi's overthrow in 2011, J79s show up frequently in news reporting as well (see here (opens new window), here (opens new window), and here (opens new window)).

Source: Jack Hurley (@loudribs) (opens new window). The original satyrical poster misidentified a J40 and three J70s as Hilux trucks. It also placed two post-2007 facelifted J79s earlier in the timeline than a pre-2007 model.

When faced with the decision of what "Toyota" vehicle to depict on the landing page of this Handbook, the Land Cruiser J45, predecessor to the J79, was chosen. This would have been the primary rugged Toyota pickup available to the Chadian and Libyan armies during the earlier stages of the Toyota War, with the J70 entering production during Operation Manta in 1984.
